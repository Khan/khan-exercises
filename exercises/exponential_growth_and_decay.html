A sample of radioactive species has a half-life of halflife period. How long, in period, will it take until only pct% of the original sample remains?

answer

Let t denote the time, in period, elapsed, and m_0 the amount of species we start out with.

Then at time t, the remaining mass is given by expr( amt ).

We'd like to know when this reaches pct/100m_0.

As an equation, pct/100m_0 = expr( amt ).

Divide through by m_0, then take the log of each side, leaving \log_2 pct/100 = - t / halflife.

Solving, t = - halflife \log_2 (pct/100), or approximately answer period.

Newton's Law of Cooling states that the difference in temperature between an object and its surroundings decays exponentially as time passes. You take a beverageTemp^\circ F cup of adjective beverage outside, where the temperature is a steady baseTemp^\circ F. After t1 minutes, the temperature of the beverage has risen_fallen to T1^\circ F. What temperature, in degrees Fahrenheit, will the beverage be after an hour?

T2

The difference in temperature is beverageTemp^\circ F - baseTemp^\circ F = beverageTemp - baseTemp^\circ F to start with.

Since the difference in temperature decays exponentially, it's given by beverageTemp - baseTempe^{-kt} for some constant k.

Combining this with the outside temperature baseTemp^\circ F, the temperature of the beverage at time t is given by baseTemp + beverageTemp - baseTempe^{-kt}.

We can use our knowledge of the temperature after t1 minutes to determine k.

Specifically, baseTemp + beverageTemp - baseTempe^{-t1k} = T1.

Solving, k = k, so the temperature of the beverage is given by baseTemp + beverageTemp - baseTempe^{-kt}
