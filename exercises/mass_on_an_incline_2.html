If the coefficient of friction between the mass and the incline is MU and the mass is released from rest, what is the acceleration of the mass due to the forces that act upon it?

((9.8*sin(INCLINE_RAD))-(MU*9.8*cos(INCLINE_RAD)))

First, define a coordinate system. It is easiest to define it in relation to the incline.

The positive x-direction will be down the incline and the positive y-direction will be perpendicular to the incline.

Then, find the forces acting on the mass.

In this case there are three forces that act on the mass. The force of gravity acts directly downward, and the force of friction acts in the opposite direction of the motion of the mass along the incline. The normal force acts upon the surface so that the block neither floats nor goes into the surface.

Break up the components of the forces to find which one causes the mass to move, which one acts against the motion, and which one is the normal force.

If we define the x-axis to be along the incline, and the y-axis to be perpendicular to the incline, the only force that must be broken down into components is gravity.The force of gravity breaks down into mg\sin(\theta), F_x, down the incline and mg\cos(\theta), F_y.

The force of friction is \mu F_n where F_n is the normal force acting on the mass.

The normal force acts perpendicularly to the surface. Since the mass is not moving in the direction that is perpendicular to the surface, the acceleration in that direction is 0. Therefore, F_n - F_y = ma = 0 and F_n = F_y.

Along the incline, F_x = mg\sin(\theta) causes the mass to move while F_f = \mu F_n acts against the motion.

Since the two forces along the incline act in different directions, their signs must be different. If we define the positive direction to be down the slope, F_{net} = F_x - \mu F_n .

Use F_{net} = ma to solve for the acceleration of the block due to gravity accounting for the friction.

Therefore, mgsin(\theta) - \mu mgcos(\theta) = ma and a = gsin(\theta) - \mu gcos(\theta) = ((9.8*sin(INCLINE_RAD))-(MU*9.8*cos(INCLINE_RAD))).toFixed(1)\ \mathrm{m/s^2}.

If the coefficient of friction between the mass and the incline is MU and the mass is released from rest, how long will it take the left corner of the mass to reach the bottom of the hill?

(sqrt((2*HEIGHT)/((9.8*pow(sin(INCLINE_RAD),2))-(9.8*MU*sin(INCLINE_RAD)*cos(INCLINE_RAD)))))

Next, find the distance it travels to reach the bottom of the hill.

Since the incline makes a right triangle with one leg equal to HEIGHT meters, we can use trigonometry to solve for the hypotenuse.

The distance traveled, d, is equal to the height divided by the sine of the incline angle, d = \frac{h}{\sin(\theta)} , or (HEIGHT/sin(INCLINE_RAD)).toFixed(3) meters.

Now use kinematic equations to find the time. Remember that the mass starts at rest.

d = v_0t + \frac{1}{2}at^2

v_0 = 0 so d = \frac{1}{2}at^2.

t = \sqrt{\dfrac{2d}{a}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{2h}{g\sin^2(\theta)- \mu g\sin(\theta)\cos(\theta)}} = (sqrt((2*HEIGHT)/((9.8*pow(sin(INCLINE_RAD),2))-(9.8*MU*sin(INCLINE_RAD)*cos(INCLINE_RAD))))).toFixed(1) s.
