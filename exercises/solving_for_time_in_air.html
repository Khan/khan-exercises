Deriving Displacement as a Function of Time, Acceleration and Initial Velocity

We want to find the time at which the displacement in the y direction is zero (when the projectile hits the ground). Since we are dealing with the y direction, we want the values in the y direction for displacement, initial velocity, and acceleration.

s_y = v_{iy}\Delta t + \dfrac{1}{2} a_y (\Delta t)^2

The y displacement will be zero, we found the initial y velocity, VELOCITYsin(ANGLE), and the y acceleration due to gravity is -9.8 m/s

0 = VELOCITY sin(ANGLE) \Delta t + \dfrac{1}{2}(G)(\Delta t)^2

0 = VELOCITY sin(ANGLE) \Delta t - -0.5*G(\Delta t)^2

Factor out a \Delta t.

0 = \Delta t (VELOCITY sin(ANGLE) - -0.5*G\Delta t)

By the zero product property, we know that:

\Delta t = 0 \space \space OR \space \space VELOCITY sin(ANGLE) - -0.5*G\Delta t = 0

Since \Delta t = 0 is when the projectile was launched, that's not what we're looking for, so we can ignore it.

VELOCITY sin(ANGLE) - -0.5*G\Delta t = 0

Add -0.5*G\Delta t to both sides.

VELOCITY sin(ANGLE) = -0.5*G\Delta t

Divide by -0.5*G.

\dfrac{VELOCITY sin(ANGLE)}{-0.5*G} = \Delta t

\Delta t \approx roundTo(1, -2*(VELOCITY * sin(ANGLE * PI / 180) / G)) seconds
