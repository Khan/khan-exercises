We know displacement is equal to average velocity times the change in time, the average velocity (with constant acceleration) is the average of the initial and final velocity, and the final velocity is equal to the initial velocity plus the acceleration times the change in time. This gives us the following equations:

1) \space \vec{s} = \vec{v}_a \cdot \Delta t

2) \space \vec{v}_a = \dfrac{\vec{v}_i+\vec{v_f}}{2}

3) \space \vec{v}_f = \vec{v}_i + \vec{a} \cdot \Delta t

Combining these three equations gives us:

\vec{s} = \vec{v_i} \Delta t + \dfrac{1}{2} \vec{a} (\Delta t)^2[Show me how]

Plug the second one into the first for \vec{v}_a.

\vec{s} = \dfrac{\vec{v}_i + \vec{v}_f}{2} \cdot \Delta t

Now put the third one in for \vec{v}_f.

\vec{s} = \dfrac{\vec{v}_i + \vec{v}_i + \vec{a} \cdot \Delta t}{2} \cdot \Delta t

\vec{s} = \dfrac{2\vec{v}_i + \vec{a} \cdot \Delta t}{2} \cdot \Delta t

\vec{s} = (\dfrac{2\vec{v}_i}{2} + \dfrac{\vec{a} \cdot \Delta t}{2}) \cdot \Delta t

\vec{s} = (\vec{v}_i + \dfrac{\vec{a} \cdot \Delta t}{2}) \cdot \Delta t

\vec{s} = \vec{v}_i \Delta t + \dfrac{\vec{a} \cdot (\Delta t)^2}{2}

\vec{s} = \vec{v}_i \Delta t + \dfrac{1}{2} \vec{a} (\Delta t)^2

Now that we have an equation for displacement as a function of time, we want to find the time at which the displacement in the y direction is zero (when the projectile hits the ground).

Let's plug in the values in the y direction for displacement, acceleration, and initial velocity.

We know that the acceleration (due to gravity) will be G\mathrm{m/s^2}, we already found the initial velocity in the y direction, VELOCITY \cdot \mathrm{sin}(ANGLE^{\circ}), and the vertical displacement will be zero.

0 = VELOCITY \space \mathrm{sin}(ANGLE^{\circ}) \Delta t + \dfrac{1}{2} (G) (\Delta t)^2

Solving for \Delta t gives us:

\Delta t = \dfrac{VELOCITY \mathrm{sin}(ANGLE^{\circ})}{-0.5 * G} \space\space \mathrm{OR} \space\space \Delta t = 0[Show me how]

0 = VELOCITY \space \mathrm{sin}(ANGLE^{\circ}) \Delta t - -0.5*G (\Delta t)^2

Factor out \Delta t.

0 = \Delta t (VELOCITY \space \mathrm{sin}(ANGLE^{\circ}) - -0.5*G \Delta t)

By the zero product property, we know:

\Delta t = 0 \space\space \mathrm{OR} \space\space VELOCITY \space \mathrm{sin}(ANGLE^{\circ}) - -0.5*G \Delta t = 0

VELOCITY \space \mathrm{sin}(ANGLE^{\circ}) - -0.5*G \Delta t = 0

VELOCITY \space \mathrm{sin}(ANGLE^{\circ}) = -0.5*G \Delta t

\dfrac{VELOCITY \space \mathrm{sin}(ANGLE^{\circ})}{-0.5*G} = \Delta t

Since \Delta t can't be 0 (that's where we started) we know that \Delta t \approx roundTo(1, (VELOCITY * sin(ANGLE * PI / 180)) / (-0.5 * G)) \space \mathrm{seconds}.
